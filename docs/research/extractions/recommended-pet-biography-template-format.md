## Recommended Pet Biography Template Format
 
| Section | Purpose | Guidance for Writers |
|---------|---------|---------------------|
| **Summary** | 3-4 sentence introduction establishing the animal's nature and strongest daily-life qualities. State ideal adopter profile. | Lead with what makes this animal special to live with, not a data inventory. Open with a general characterization ("makes a relaxed companion") then provide specific behavioral examples. Avoid leading with weight, breed, or command lists. |
| **Training & Activity** | Commands known, reliability level, learning speed, exercise needs, energy level, preferred activities. Include any training support programs. | List solid commands first, then commands in progress with honest reliability ("come and drop work half the time"). Use selective metrics where helpful. Describe energy level through observable behavior ("relaxes during the day and settles nearby when you work"). |
| **Home Behavior** | Indoor settling, house training status, alone-time tolerance, behavior around household activity, handling tolerance. Verified in-home observations are high value. | Note if behavior has been observed in foster care. Group related behaviors: settling/chewing, distractions/boundaries, overnight/elimination. Use concrete durations for house training claims. |
| **Household Fit** | Compatibility with dogs, cats, and children consolidated. Requirements and restrictions. | Describe dog behavior on walks and in play. State requirements clearly ("A meet-and-greet with any resident dog is required"). For untested areas, state plainly ("has not been tested with children; adopters with children should request a supervised introduction"). |
| **Adoption Support** | Behaviors that may require patience or training during adjustment period. Training support available. Frame challenges as trainable with resources offered. | Open with trainability statement. List specific support included with adoption. For each challenge: name it, describe current state, add prospective language ("is improving," "will develop"). Keep each item to 1-2 sentences. |
 
---
 
### Structure Rationale
 
**Five consolidated sections** reduce word count and prevent redundancy. Separate Dog/Cat/Child compatibility sections inflate length without adding value.
 
**Section order** leads with attractive qualities (Summary → Training & Activity → Home Behavior), then requirements (Household Fit), then challenges (Adoption Support). Adopters skim; hook them before filtering.
 
**"Adoption Support" reframe** positions challenges as temporary items with professional resources, rather than deficits. This aligns with research on negative affect (β = .08, p < .001) while maintaining honest disclosure.
 
---
 
### Writing Style
 
**Warm but behavioral:** Convey appealing qualities through observable actions. "Rests her head on your lap when you sit down" communicates affection without social descriptors like "sweet" or "loving."
 
**Narrative flow:** Each section should read as connected prose, not a checklist of observations. Group related behaviors into flowing sentences.
 
**Selective metrics:** Use concrete numbers where they aid decision-making (house training duration, exercise needs, command reliability). Do not over-quantify. The Summary should read naturally.
 
**Current behaviors only:** Describe what adopters will experience. If a behavior required training but is now resolved, describe only the current state ("calm for harnessing" not "required desensitization training but is now calm for harnessing").
 
**Prospective language:** Use future-focused framing in Adoption Support ("will develop," "is improving," "will fade with time").
 
---
 
### Disclosure Guidance
 
**In profile:** Current behaviors, verified observations, household requirements, challenges that are trainable with support offered.
 
**Reserved for meet-and-greet:** Isolated incidents requiring context (e.g., single resource guarding event), nuanced behavioral history, detailed management strategies.
 
Not everything belongs in the public profile. Some information is better delivered in person where staff can provide context and answer questions.
 
---
 
### Word Count
 
Target 200-280 words for website biographies. If a profile exceeds this range, look for:
- Redundancy across sections
- Over-quantification
- Extinct behaviors included
- Separate compatibility sections that could be consolidated
 
---
 
### Common Mistakes to Avoid
 
| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|------------|
| Leading Summary with weight/breed/commands | Lead with distinctive daily-life quality |
| Social descriptors ("sweet," "loving," "friendly") | Behavioral descriptions ("rests head on your lap") |
| Including extinct/resolved behaviors | Only describe current state |
| Over-quantifying ("learns in 3-5 repetitions") | Use metrics selectively |
| Separate Dog/Cat/Child sections | Consolidate into Household Fit |
| Clinical/robotic tone | Balance research compliance with warmth through behavioral language |
| Checklist-style observations | Flowing narrative sentences |
| Disclosing everything in profile | Reserve context-dependent items for meet-and-greet |
| Redundancy (mentioning same program 3x) | State once in most relevant section |
| Vague challenge framing | Specific challenge + prospective language + support offered |
 
---
 
### Example Summary Patterns
 
**Avoid:**
> Sally is a 44-pound pit mix who knows sit, down, come, look, touch, and drop, and learns new cues within a few repetitions.
 
**Prefer:**
> Sally is a young mixed breed dog who makes a relaxed companion. She rests her head on your lap when you sit down and settles nearby when you work.
 
The first leads with data. The second leads with what life with this dog feels like.
