# Key Adoption Profile Research Findings Summary
 
### Source Studies
 
**Markowitz (2020)** analyzed 184,805 Petfinder profiles plus experimental validation (N = 987) and Twitter engagement (N = 3,245 tweets). **Kelling et al.** surveyed 561 participants viewing fictional dog advertisements with systematically varied characteristics.
 
### Central Finding
 
Pet adoption persuasion operates through the **central route** of the Elaboration Likelihood Model. Profiles succeed when they provide concrete, analytic information that helps adopters evaluate fit—not when they rely on emotional appeals or humanizing language.
 
---
 
### Statistical Findings (Markowitz 2020)
 
| Variable | Effect | Significance |
|----------|--------|--------------|
| Analytic writing style | Shorter time online | β = -.01, p = .001 |
| Social/humanizing words | Longer time online | β = .05, p < .001 |
| Social words | Lower adoption rate | OR = .94, p < .001 |
| Ingestion words (eating, drinking) | Shorter duration | β = -.03, p < .001 |
| Future-focused language | Shorter duration | β = -.02, p < .001 |
| Negative affect | Longer time online | β = .08, p < .001 |
| Photos | Longer duration | β = .04, p < .001 |
 
### Key Findings (Kelling et al.)
 
- Participants preferred **paragraph format over bullet points** (p = 0.01)
- **No preference** between "cute" vs. "plain" language styles
- **BUT** participants reading "plain" ads identified behavioral challenges significantly better:
  - Housetraining: 83.9% (plain) vs. 57.7% (cute), p < .001
  - Dog aggression: 87.5% (plain) vs. 52.2% (cute), p < .001
- **Behavior rated most important** information by participants (mean rank 1.97, 50.5% ranked it #1)
- 24.6% of participants specifically requested more behavioral/personality information

## Top 10 Do's
 
1. **Write concrete behavioral descriptions.** "Sits at your feet while you work" beats "loves being near you." Ingestion words describing eating, drinking, and chewing behaviors predicted shorter durations online (β = -.03, p < .001).
 
2. **Use articles and prepositions liberally.** "The dog waits by the door at mealtimes" demonstrates analytic style. High rates of articles ("a," "the") and prepositions signal complex, categorical thinking and correlate with faster adoption.
 
3. **Describe observable actions and activities.** What the pet does daily: eating habits, play preferences, sleep locations, greeting behaviors. Action verbs outperform personality inferences.
 
4. **Frame the future.** Use words like "will thrive in," "would do well with," "looking for a home where." Future-focused language helps adopters imagine life with the pet (β = -.02, p < .001).
 
5. **Provide decision-relevant information.** Energy level, training status, compatibility with children/other pets, medical needs. Participants rated behavior as the most important information category.
 
6. **Use paragraph format over bullet points.** Participants showed significant preference for paragraph advertisements (p = 0.01), likely because they read as more personal and complete.
 
7. **State behavioral challenges plainly.** "Tends to display aggression toward other dogs" was understood by 87.5% of readers. "Is picky about his friends" was understood by only 52.2%. Clarity prevents adoption failures.
 
8. **Include house training status with specifics.** "Fully house-trained" or "does best on a schedule with regular outdoor access." Readers specifically sought this information.
 
9. **Use analytic, complex sentence structures.** Profiles with more markers of analytic thinking (articles, prepositions) and fewer storytelling words (pronouns, adverbs, conjunctions) correlate with faster adoption.
 
10. **Convey warmth through behavior, not labels.** "Rests her head on your lap when you sit down" communicates affection through observable action rather than social descriptors.
 
---
 
## Top 10 Don'ts
 
1. **Avoid humanizing social descriptors.** Cut "sweetheart," "soulmate," "baby girl," "handsome boy," "gentleman," "princess." Social words predicted longer time online (β = .05, p < .001) and lower adoption rates (OR = .94, p < .001).
 
2. **Don't rely on emotional appeals.** "Please give this angel a chance" underperforms factual descriptions. Peripheral cues like emotional pleas are less effective than central-route information processing.
 
3. **Avoid pronouns over nouns.** "She loves it" is weaker than "Luna loves fetch with tennis balls." Pronouns are associated with storytelling (peripheral route) rather than analytic writing (central route).
 
4. **Don't include negativity about temperament.** Any negativity extended time online (β = .08, p < .001). Reframe challenges as requirements: "Needs experienced handler" not "Can be difficult."
 
5. **Don't pad with generic filler.** Every sentence should add valuable information. Word count positively correlated with time online, suggesting longer profiles without substance hurt adoption.
 
6. **Don't use "cute" euphemisms for challenges.** "Gets the zoomies" was less clear than "high energy." "Picky about his friends" obscured dog aggression. Only 52-58% of readers understood euphemistic language.
 
7. **Don't omit why the dog is at the shelter.** 8% of participants specifically noted wanting this information. Omission may trigger suspicion; direct disclosure allows adopters to evaluate fit.
 
8. **Don't over-rely on photos.** More photos per profile predicted longer duration online (β = .04, p < .001). Photos may reduce uncertainty but don't replace behavioral information.
 
9. **Avoid first-person "cute" voice.** Writing as if the dog is speaking ("My name is Tex and I am about 3 years old") did not increase preference and may reduce clarity of important information.
 
10. **Don't assume jargon is understood.** Terms like "zoomies" were not universally recognized. Use plain language that any potential adopter can understand without specialized knowledge.
 
---
 
## Practical Implications
 
The research converges on a counterintuitive conclusion: **warmth and emotional appeal hurt adoption outcomes, while concrete behavioral information helps.** This likely occurs because:
 
- Adopters process profiles through the central route, seeking information to evaluate fit
- Social descriptors signal that the writer is "selling" rather than informing, triggering skepticism
- Concrete behavioral descriptions reduce uncertainty and allow better self-selection
- Clear disclosure of challenges prevents adoption failures and returns
